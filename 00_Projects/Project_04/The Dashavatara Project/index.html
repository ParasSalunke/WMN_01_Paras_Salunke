Lord Vishnu

Lord Vishnu, one of the principal deities in Hinduism, is revered as the preserver and protector of the universe. He forms part of the Hindu trinity, known as the Trimurti, alongside Brahma (the creator) and Shiva (the destroyer). While Brahma is responsible for the creation of the world, and Shiva for its eventual destruction, Vishnu's role is to maintain balance, order, and harmony within the cosmos, ensuring that righteousness (dharma) is upheld. Vishnu is often depicted as a divine being with a serene and blue complexion, symbolizing the infinite and boundless sky. He typically has four arms, each holding significant items: a conch shell (shankha), representing the sound of creation; a discus (chakra), symbolizing the mind and the cycle of time; a mace (gada), signifying strength and power; and a lotus (padma), which represents purity, beauty, and the unfolding of spiritual consciousness. Vishnu is usually shown resting on the cosmic serpent Shesha, floating on the ocean of milk, with his consort, Goddess Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth and prosperity, seated at his feet. A key aspect of Vishnu's role as a preserver is his ability to incarnate in different forms, known as avatars, whenever the world is threatened by chaos, evil, or adharma (unrighteousness). These incarnations, collectively known as the Dashavatara, include ten major avatars: Matsya (fish), Kurma (turtle), Varaha (boar), Narasimha (half-man, half-lion), Vamana (dwarf), Parashurama (warrior with an axe), Rama (prince of Ayodhya), Krishna (divine statesman and charioteer), Buddha (the enlightened one), and Kalki (the future warrior who will appear at the end of the current age). Each avatar represents a specific period in history when Vishnu intervened to restore balance, protect the virtuous, and destroy evil forces. Vishnu's two most popular incarnations are Rama, the hero of the epic Ramayana, and Krishna, a central figure in the Mahabharata. Krishna's teachings to Arjuna in the Bhagavad Gita emphasize the importance of duty, devotion, and righteousness, making them foundational texts in Hindu philosophy.
